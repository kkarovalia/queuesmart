# **Development Methodology \- QueueSmart**

## **Methodology: Scrum (Agile)**

For QueueSmart, Scrum is the development methodology to adhere to. It is a specific Agile methodology which involves rapid and adaptive work, and the reason it is a good fit here is because of the nature of the assignment itself. We're going to be given feedback that essentially simulates what it's like to receive descriptions from customers on what the app should do, and on top of that, we're delivering this project in stages, or increments. That already lines up with how Scrum works. It's built around sprints, and the assignments already do this for us. A1, A2, A3, A4 each act like their own sprint with its own goal and deadline, so our team doesn’t even have to make up a sprint schedule, as it is already laid out by the course.

As requirements evolve, Scrum lets us adjust the design instead of locking us into decisions we made early on when we understood the project the least. For example, something we assume in A1 about the architecture might not hold up once we start designing the UI in A2 properly, and Scrum gives us room to fix that without it feeling like we are stuck driving into the same pitfalls. It also doesn't demand a ton of overhead. Something like a quick check-in or a short review after finishing each assignment is enough to stay on track, which matters when there's other coursework. Each assignment also comes with pretty clear grading criteria, like A1 needing a diagram and a methodology writeup, and that maps well onto Scrum's idea of having a clear end point for each sprint. On top of that, since we have to document contributions and TAs are going to check GitHub history, doing a short retrospective after each assignment gives a natural point to actually write down who did what, instead of trying to remember it all at the end of the semester.

## **How This Helps Across Multiple Assignments**

Since A1 through A4 all build on each other, treating each one like its own sprint means we can go back and adjust earlier decisions instead of being stuck with them. If something about the architecture from A1 doesn't quite work once we get into the UI for A2, that's fine. We have the ability to revise it instead of treating it as locked in. It also keeps a consistent rhythm going, having us plan, build, review, adjust, that should carry naturally into the final project and demo, and it keeps the contribution documentation as an ongoing thing rather than something we have to reconstruct after the fact.
